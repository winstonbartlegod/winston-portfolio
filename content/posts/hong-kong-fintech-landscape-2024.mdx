People outside Hong Kong often misunderstand its financial centre status. They see the geopolitical headlines, the talent migration numbers, and they assume the story is one of decline. From inside the city — working at EY's financial services practice, advising banks navigating the HKMA's new AI governance frameworks — the story looks considerably more complicated.

## The HKMA's Quiet Revolution

The Hong Kong Monetary Authority has been methodically building one of the most sophisticated AI governance frameworks in the world, and almost nobody outside the industry is paying attention.

The **Model Risk Management Supervisory Policy Manual** (the "MRM SPM") — published in 2021 and substantially updated in 2023 — requires banks to:

1. Maintain comprehensive model inventories (yes, including ML models)
2. Conduct independent model validation for material models
3. Document bias and fairness assessments for models used in credit decisions
4. Demonstrate explainability for regulatory review

This is not EU AI Act boilerplate. This is detailed, operational guidance written by people who understand how neural networks work. It's also creating an enormous demand for exactly the skills the local talent market has in short supply: people who can build ML models *and* understand why a regulator cares about SHAP values.

<Callout type="info">
The HKMA's **Fintech 2025** strategy explicitly calls for "full banking digitalisation" by 2025 — including AI-driven credit underwriting, real-time anti-money laundering surveillance, and AI-assisted supervisory examinations of banks. They're not bluffing.
</Callout>

## The Virtual Bank Experiment: Results After 4 Years

Hong Kong's eight licensed virtual banks (ZA Bank, Mox, Airstar, WeLab, etc.) have now been operating for several years. The takeaways are nuanced:

**What worked:**
- Customer onboarding via eKYC (SFC and HKMA both accept facial recognition + document OCR, removing the need for branch visits)
- NLP-driven customer service (Mox's AI chatbot handles ~70% of tier-1 queries without human escalation)
- Real-time fraud scoring using behavioural biometrics

**What didn't:**
- Deposit gathering: virtual banks collectively hold <2% of HK retail deposits. The HSBC brand still wins on trust.
- Mortgage and complex lending: pure digital underwriting hits regulatory limits on documentation requirements

The virtual bank experiment has effectively been a large-scale pilot for technologies that traditional banks are now scrambling to adopt.

## The NFT/Digital Asset Hangover

I spent part of 2022-2023 advising Tencent WeChat Pay HK on NFT strategy. The timing was terrible (the NFT market peaked in early 2022) but the regulatory analysis remains relevant.

Hong Kong's SFC moved early to establish a licensing regime for Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) under the Anti-Money Laundering Ordinance. Key requirements:

- AML/CFT obligations equivalent to traditional financial institutions
- Custody requirements for client digital assets (≥98% cold storage)
- Regular external audits of proof-of-reserves

The regulatory clarity that looks "burdensome" to crypto purists is exactly what institutional capital needs before it can flow into the space. Singapore went the other way (initially more permissive) and ended up with the Three Arrows Capital and Genesis collapses happening on its doorstep. Hong Kong's conservatism looks prescient in retrospect.

## What's Actually Interesting in 2024

The most underappreciated development in Hong Kong's FinTech scene right now is **Generative AI for regulatory compliance**. The compliance function at major banks is massively overstaffed relative to its output — junior lawyers and compliance officers spend enormous amounts of time reading regulatory circulars, comparing them to policy documents, and updating procedures.

Large language models are extraordinarily good at exactly this task. The HKMA has published >200 circulars in the last three years. GPT-4 with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) can answer "does circular X require us to update policy Y?" in seconds, with citations.

The productivity implication is staggering — and the headcount implication is something the banks are thinking very carefully about.

## My Honest Assessment

Hong Kong will not become "the crypto capital of Asia" — that ship has sailed and Singapore, Dubai, and increasingly the UAE are ahead on that specific dimension. But on **institutional AI in financial services** — model risk governance, AI-driven credit decisions, regulatory technology — Hong Kong is arguably ahead of every other financial centre except London, and possibly ahead of London on regulatory clarity for ML specifically.

That's a more modest claim than "FinTech hub," but it's a real and durable one. And it's the market I happen to work in every day.
