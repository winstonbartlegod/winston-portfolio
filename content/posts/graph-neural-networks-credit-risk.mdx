Credit risk modelling has a dirty secret: the models most banks still rely on — logistic regression scorecards, decision trees, even vanilla gradient boosting — were designed in an era when the richest thing you knew about a borrower was their FICO score and their debt-to-income ratio.

That era is over.

## The Problem with Tabular Models

Consider a small business loan application. The applicant looks fine on paper: reasonable cash flow, moderate leverage, clean credit history. But what if their three largest customers are themselves in financial distress? What if their primary supplier just filed for Chapter 11?

A tabular model — one that sees only the applicant's row in the database — is blind to all of this. It treats each borrower as an isolated island.

Reality is a network.

## Borrowers as Nodes, Relationships as Edges

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) were designed precisely for this situation. Instead of modelling each entity in isolation, a GNN constructs a graph where:

- **Nodes** represent entities: borrowers, corporates, counterparties, collateral assets
- **Edges** represent relationships: supplier-customer ties, ownership structures, shared directors, transaction flows

Information propagates through this graph in message-passing rounds. After $k$ rounds, each node's representation encodes information from its $k$-hop neighbourhood — exactly the kind of systemic, contagion-aware signal that traditional models miss.

```python
import torch
from torch_geometric.nn import GATConv

class CreditGAT(torch.nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, in_channels: int, hidden: int, heads: int = 4):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv1 = GATConv(in_channels, hidden, heads=heads, dropout=0.3)
        self.conv2 = GATConv(hidden * heads, hidden, heads=1, concat=False)
        self.classifier = torch.nn.Linear(hidden, 1)

    def forward(self, x, edge_index):
        x = torch.nn.functional.elu(self.conv1(x, edge_index))
        x = self.conv2(x, edge_index)
        return torch.sigmoid(self.classifier(x)).squeeze(-1)
```

<Callout type="tip">
The Graph Attention Network (GAT) is particularly well-suited to credit networks because its attention mechanism can learn to weight supplier relationships more heavily than, say, a distant equity investor — without you having to hard-code that domain knowledge.
</Callout>

## Building the Knowledge Graph

For my MSc thesis at HKUST, I constructed a synthetic borrower graph mirroring the HKMA's stress-test scenarios:

1. **Corporate borrowers** (nodes with financial statement features)
2. **Supplier-customer edges** (from trade finance data)
3. **Ownership edges** (UBO registry data)
4. **Collateral linkages** (shared property assets)

After three message-passing rounds, the GAT achieved an **AUC of 0.893** versus **0.811** for an equivalent XGBoost model trained on the same node features — a 10% lift that translates to meaningful capital savings at bank scale.

## What About Explainability?

Regulators rightly ask: "Can you explain this decision?" GNNs have an advantage here that's often overlooked. Attention weights from GATConv give you a direct, per-edge measure of how much each relationship contributed to the risk score.

For a flagged loan, you can say: "This borrower's default probability spiked because their top customer (node 47) shows elevated distress signals — and node 47 accounts for 73% of their revenue (high attention weight on that edge)."

That's a story. Traditional models give you SHAP values on tabular features — useful, but far less actionable for a credit officer.

## The Road Ahead

The banking industry is sitting on rich graph-structured data — correspondent banking networks, payment graphs, supply chains — that almost none of it is using for risk modelling. The barriers are mostly organisational (data silos) and cultural (unfamiliarity with graph ML), not technical.

That's going to change. The question is whether your institution leads or follows.
